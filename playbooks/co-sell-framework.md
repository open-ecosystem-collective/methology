# Co-Sell Framework

**Open Ecosystem Collective – How to Win Deals Together**

Co-selling means working with a software vendor or a complementary company
to win a deal neither of you would have won alone.

For small Cloud Native companies, co-sell is one of the highest-leverage
growth activities available – and one of the least used.

**For:** Companies with Assessment score 31–45 (Co-Sell Maturity D3 ≥ 8)  
**Delivered by:** Level 3+  
**License:** CC-BY-SA 4.0

---

## Two types of co-sell

**Vendor co-sell**  
You work with a software vendor (AWS, HashiCorp, Datadog, etc.)
to sell their product plus your services together.
The vendor brings leads or lends credibility; you bring implementation capability.

**Partner co-sell**  
You work with a complementary company (different but adjacent services)
to serve a client neither of you can serve alone.
Example: you do Kubernetes platform engineering; they do security.
Same client, different workstreams, shared opportunity.

Both are valuable. Start with whichever comes naturally from your existing relationships.

## Part 1 – Vendor co-sell

### Step 1 – Register as a co-sell partner

Most major vendors have a formal co-sell programme within their partner portal.

| Vendor | Programme name | Where to register |
|---|---|---|
| AWS | ISV Accelerate / APN Co-Sell | APN Partner Central → Co-Sell |
| Google Cloud | Partner Advantage Co-Sell | Google Partner Portal → Referrals |
| Microsoft Azure | One Commercial Partner | Microsoft Partner Center |
| HashiCorp | Technology Partner Programme | partner.hashicorp.com |
| Datadog | Partner Network Co-Sell | Datadog Partner Portal |

Registration typically requires: active partner status, at least one certification,
and a brief capability description.

### Step 2 – Share your ideal customer profile

Once registered, give your vendor contact a clear picture of who you want:

> **[Company name] serves:** Mid-size companies (50–500 employees) in the
> DACH region running or migrating to cloud-native infrastructure on
> [vendor] platform. They typically have 3–15 engineers and no dedicated
> platform team. Their primary pain is [specific pain].

The more specific this is, the better the leads you receive.
A vague profile produces vague leads.

### Step 3 – Register your opportunities

When you are working on a deal where the vendor's product is involved,
register it in their co-sell portal. This does several things:

- It alerts the vendor's field team that a deal is in progress
- It may trigger their involvement (introductions, technical support, reference calls)
- It builds your track record for MDF applications

**How to register:**  
Go to the vendor partner portal → Opportunities → Register new opportunity.
Include: company name, deal size estimate, stage, and your role.

### Step 4 – Request vendor involvement when it helps

Not every deal needs active vendor involvement. But for deals where
the vendor's credibility or resources would help, ask directly:

> *"We have an opportunity at [company type] for [vendor product + our services].
> The client has technical questions about [topic]. Could you join a 30-minute
> call to speak to the product roadmap?"*

Vendors want to help close deals involving their product.
They rarely offer unless asked. Ask.

### Step 5 – Close and report

After the deal closes:
- Update the opportunity in the partner portal
- Ask the client if they are willing to be a reference
- Inform your vendor contact – a closed deal strengthens your relationship
- Document it as a case study (anonymised if needed) for future MDF applications

## Part 2 – Partner co-sell

### Step 1 – Map your partner landscape

Identify companies that serve the same clients as you without competing:

| Company | What they do | Our overlap | Complementary to us? |
|---|---|---|---|
| | | | ○ Yes ○ No ○ Maybe |
| | | | ○ Yes ○ No ○ Maybe |
| | | | ○ Yes ○ No ○ Maybe |

**Sources for finding complementary companies:**
- CNCF Landscape – find companies in adjacent categories
- KubeCon exhibitor lists – who is solving different problems for the same audience
- Your own lost deals – who won the parts you did not?
- Your existing clients – who else do they work with?

### Step 2 – Start with one conversation, not a formal agreement

Reach out to one or two companies with a simple message:

> *"We have been seeing [company name] in the CNCF community and think
> we solve complementary problems for similar clients. Would you be open
> to a 30-minute conversation to explore if there is overlap?"*

No partnership deck. No legal agreement yet. Just a conversation.

In the conversation, cover:
- What you do and for whom
- What they do and for whom  
- Have you ever been in the same deal?
- Are there deals you each wish the other had been involved in?

### Step 3 – Define the co-sell trigger

If there is genuine complementarity, define when you would call each other:

> **[Partner company] calls [your company] when:**  
> A client needs [your capability] alongside [their capability].

> **[Your company] calls [partner company] when:**  
> A client needs [their capability] alongside [your capability].

Write this down and share it with the relevant people at both companies.
A trigger that lives only in the CEO's head does not work.

### Step 4 – Run a pilot deal together

Find one real opportunity where both companies participate.
Keep it simple for the first deal. Define upfront:
- Who leads the client relationship?
- How are commercial conversations handled?
- What happens if only one of you wins?

Review the experience together after the deal closes or stalls.
Use that review to decide whether to formalise the relationship.

### Step 5 – Formalise only when it is working

A formal partner agreement is worth creating only after you have
completed at least one successful joint deal. Before that, the
administrative overhead is not worth it.

When you do formalise, keep the agreement simple:
- Referral fee structure (if any)
- Who owns the client relationship in joint deals
- How leads are shared and tracked
- Confidentiality expectations

## Common co-sell mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---|---|
| Waiting for the vendor to bring you leads | Register opportunities proactively; leads come to active partners |
| Over-formalising too early | One conversation and one pilot deal before any agreement |
| Not registering deals | Every registered deal builds your track record |
| Choosing partners by friendship, not fit | Map complementarity first; relationships follow |
| No follow-up after a closed deal | Every close is an asset – case study, reference, reporting |
